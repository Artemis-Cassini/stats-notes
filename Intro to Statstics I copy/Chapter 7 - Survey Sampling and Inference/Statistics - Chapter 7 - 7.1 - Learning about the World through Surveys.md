### Survey Terminology
- A **population** is a group of objects of people we wish to study
- We wish to know the value of a **parameter**, a numerical value that characterizes some aspect of this population
- A **census** is a survey in which every member of the population it measured
- A **sample** is a collection of people or objects taken from the population of interest 
- A **statistic** is a numerical characteristic of a sample of data
- Statistics are sometimes called **estimators**, and the numbers that result are called **estimates**
- **Statistical inference** is the science of drawing conclusions about a population based on observing only a small subset of that population

| Statistics (based on data)      | Parameters (typically unknown)            |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Sample Mean - x̄ (x-bar)        | Population Mean - μ (mu)                  |
| Sample Standard Deviation - *s* | Population Standard Deviation - σ (sigma) |
| Sample Variance - *s^2*         | Population Variance - σ^2                 |
| Sample Proportion - p̂ (p-hat)  | Population Proportion - *p*               |

### What Could Possibly Go Wrong? The Problem of Bias
- A method is **biased** if it has a tendency to produce, on average, the wrong value
- The first is through **sampling bias**, which results from taking a sample that is not representative of the population
- A second way is **measurement bias**, which comes from asking questions that do not produce a true answer

### Simple Random Sampling Saves the Day
- A random sample does not mean that we stand on a street corner and stop whomever we like to ask them to participate in our survey (Statisticians call this a **convenience sample**, for obvious reasons)
- One basic method that's easy to understand (but somewhat difficult to put into practice) is **simple random sampling (SRS)**
- **Without replacement** means that once a subject is selected for a sample, that subject cannot be selected again

#### Sampling in Practice
- **Nonresponse Bias** can still be a problem, and the possibility always exists that methods of taking the random sample are flawed (as they are if only landline telephones are used when many in the population use cell phones)
