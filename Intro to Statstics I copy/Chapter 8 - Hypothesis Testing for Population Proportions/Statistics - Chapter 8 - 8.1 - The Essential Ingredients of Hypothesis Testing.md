- **Hypothesis testing** is a procedure that enables us to choose between two claims when we have variability in our measurements

### Main Ingredient - A Pair of Hypotheses
- The claim that the spun coin is fair is probably not that outlandish to you. Such a hypothesis is called a **null hypothesis**. Our null hypothesis is that p = 0.50.
- One the other hand, the hypothesis that we hope to convince the world is true is called the **alternative hypothesis**. Outs is p ≠ 0.5
- The **null hypothesis**, which we write H0 (and pronounce "H-naught" or simply "the null hypothesis"), is the neutral, status quo. The null hypothesis will always have an = sign
- The **alternative hypothesis**, Ha (pronounced "H-A"), is the research hypothesis. It is a statement about the value of a parameter that we intend to demonstrate is true
	- H0: p = 0.5
	- Ha: p ≠ 0.5
- Therefore, he believes the proportion of married adults is less than the historical value of 0.70. Such hypotheses are called **one-sided hypotheses**
- A hypothesis with a “not equal to” sign is called a **two-sided hypothesis**

| Two-Sided  | One-Sided (Left) | One-Sided (Right) |
| ---------- | ---------------- | ----------------- |
| H0: p = p0 | H0: p = p0       | H0: p = p0        |
| Ha: p ≠ p0 | Ha: p < p0       | Ha: p > p0        |
### Add In: Making Mistakes
- The **significant level** is the name for a special probability: it is the probability of making the mistake of rejecting the null hypothesis when, in fact, the null hypothesis is true

### Mix With: The Test Statistic
- A **test statistic** compares the real world with the null hypothesis world
- To make the comparison, we use the **one-proportion z-test statistic**
